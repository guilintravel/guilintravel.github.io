Guilin in February: Chinese New Year Travel Tips

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

Imagine a landscape so surreal it feels painted by an artist’s delicate brush—towering limestone karsts shrouded in a soft morning mist, serene rivers winding through emerald-green fields, and a sense of tranquility that permeates the very air. This is Guilin, a place often hailed as one of China’s most breathtaking destinations. Now, picture this natural masterpiece during its most vibrant, culturally rich time of the year: the Chinese New Year, or Spring Festival. Visiting Guilin in February is an experience that transcends typical tourism; it is an immersion into a world where ancient traditions, familial warmth, and stunning natural beauty converge to create something truly magical. February marks the heart of winter, but in subtropical Guilin, the cold is mild, often crisp and refreshing, adding a layer of charm to the already picturesque scenery. The city, however, undergoes a dramatic transformation. The usual calm gives way to a festive frenzy, red lanterns adorn every street, the aroma of special holiday foods fills the air, and the spirit of renewal is palpable. This guide will navigate you through the unique joys and practicalities of experiencing Guilin during this incredible season, ensuring your journey is as smooth as it is unforgettable.

Embracing the Spring Festival Atmosphere

The Chinese New Year is not a single day but a period of celebration, typically spanning over 15 days, with the Eve and the first three days being the most significant. In 2024, the Lunar New Year begins on February 10th, ushering in the Year of the Dragon. Guilin, with its deep-rooted traditions, celebrates with immense fervor. The entire city is awash in a sea of red—the color symbolizing good fortune and joy. You'll see intricate paper cuttings adorning windows, couplets (Duilian) pasted on doorways with poetic wishes for the new year, and the constant, cheerful sound of firecrackers (though increasingly regulated in urban centers) echoing through the streets. The atmosphere is one of collective happiness and excitement. For a traveler, simply walking through the streets of Guilin or the smaller towns like Xingping is a cultural spectacle. The sense of community is strong, and while you are an observer, the warmth extended to visitors during this time is often genuine and touching.

Must-Experience Festive Events

While in Guilin during the Spring Festival, make a point to participate in or witness these local traditions:

New Year’s Eve Reunion Dinner (Nianye Fan): While this is primarily a family event, some local restaurants offer special festive meals for tourists. It’s a feast featuring symbolic dishes like fish (for abundance), dumplings (Jiaozi, resembling ancient gold ingots for wealth), and niangao (a sticky rice cake for a higher year).

Temple Fairs (Miao Hui): Around the days following New Year’s Day, temple fairs spring up in parks and near historic sites. The Seven Star Park often hosts a vibrant fair. Here, you can watch traditional performances like lion dances and dragon dances, sample an array of street food, and shop for unique handicrafts and souvenirs.

Lantern Festival (Yuanxiao Jie): Marking the end of the New Year celebrations on the 15th day of the first lunar month, this is a visual delight. In the evening, people release beautiful lanterns into the sky or onto the rivers. The Li River or Shanhu Lake in Guilin can offer a stunning backdrop for this luminous spectacle.

Navigating the Practicalities: Travel Tips for the High Season

Traveling during Chinese New Year, also known as Chunyun, the spring migration, is a double-edged sword. You witness incredible culture, but you also contend with the largest annual human migration on the planet. Millions of people travel to their hometowns, making transportation and accommodation a challenge. Forewarned is forearmed.

Booking and Transportation

Plan and Book Extremely Early: This is non-negotiable. You should book your flights, high-speed train tickets, and hotels at least 2-3 months in advance. Prices will be at their peak, and availability will disappear quickly. Trains are especially packed; using a travel agent to secure tickets can be worth the service fee.

Embrace Patience: Airports, train stations, and long-distance bus stations will be chaotic. Expect crowds, long lines, and potential delays. Pack your patience and a good book. Allow for extra time for every journey.

Internal Travel: While the Li River cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo operates, schedules might be altered. Taxis and ride-hailing apps like Didi will be in high demand and less available. Renting a bicycle or an e-bike to explore the Yangshuo countryside remains a fantastic and flexible option.

Accommodation and Closures

Hotel Choices: Stay in a hotel rather than a hostel for better service and guaranteed heating, as February can be chilly. Many smaller, family-run guesthouses might close for the first few days of the festival as the owners celebrate with their families. Larger international chains are your most reliable bet.

Expect Closures: Do not be surprised if many small local restaurants, shops, and even some attractions have reduced hours or are completely closed, especially on New Year’s Eve and the first two days of the New Year. Major tourist sites like the Reed Flute Cave and Elephant Trunk Hill will likely be open, but they will also be exceptionally crowded with domestic tourists.

Plan Your Meals: Have a backup plan for food. Stock up on snacks from a supermarket beforehand. Major restaurants attached to hotels will be open, but independent eateries may be shut. This is also a great opportunity to try convenience store snacks—you might discover a new favorite!

Iconic Sights Without the Usual Crowds (Well, Almost)

The irony of visiting during the national holiday is that Guilin’s natural sights are both more crowded in terms of people, yet potentially less crowded in terms of organized tour groups from the West. The key is to time your visits strategically.

The Li River Cruise

The iconic journey from Guilin to Yangshuo is a must-do, regardless of the season. In February, the river is often quieter, and the misty conditions create an ethereal, almost mystical atmosphere. The karst mountains appear to float on the clouds, offering phenomenal photo opportunities. While the boats will be busy with Chinese tourists, the sheer beauty is undiminished. Book your cruise tickets as part of your overall travel package to avoid disappointment.

Yangshuo and the Yulong River

The town of Yangshuo is the gateway to some of the most stunning countryside in China. While the main West Street (Xi Jie) will be packed with festive revelers, venturing just a few minutes out of town brings peace. A bamboo raft ride on the calmer Yulong River is often a more serene experience than the Li River. Cycling through the fields, past ancient bridges and rural villages, is absolute magic. You’ll see farmers preparing for the spring planting and might even catch glimpses of local families celebrating together.

Longji Rice Terraces

A trip to the Longji Terraces (Dragon’s Backbone) in February is a unique experience. The terraces are not filled with water or green rice shoots; instead, you might be lucky enough to see them dusted with a rare layer of frost or snow, creating stark, beautiful patterns against the earth. The ethnic Yao and Zhuang villages are particularly fascinating during the New Year, as their traditional celebrations, including unique dances and clothing, are in full swing. The hiking trails will be less crowded than in autumn or spring, but be warned: the paths can be slippery, so wear sturdy, non-slip shoes.

Gastronomic Adventures: A Festival for Your Taste Buds

Chinese New Year is all about food, and Guilin’s cuisine is a highlight of any visit. The festive period brings out special dishes and treats.

Guilin Rice Noodles (Guilin Mifen): This is the city’s signature breakfast dish. Don’t miss it. The standard way is to eat the noodles dry with toppings like peanuts, pickled vegetables, and chili, then drink the broth afterwards. It’s the perfect warm-up on a chilly February morning.

Festive Specialties: Look for Niangao (sweet or savory sticky rice cakes), Yusheng (a raw fish salad for prosperity, more common in southern China), and various dumplings. Street vendors at temple fairs are your best bet for trying a wide variety.

Beer Fish (Pijiu Yu): A famous dish from Yangshuo, where fish from the Li River is cooked with local beer, tomatoes, and spices. It’s a rich, flavorful must-try.

Osmanthus Cake: Guilin is known for its osmanthus flowers. This sweet, fragrant cake is a lovely local delicacy often enjoyed during celebrations.

Packing and Cultural Etiquette

What to Wear: February in Guilin is cold and damp. The average temperature ranges from 5°C to 10°C (41°F to 50°F). Pack layers—thermal underwear, sweaters, a warm jacket, a scarf, and a waterproof windbreaker are essential. Good walking shoes with grip are a must for navigating slippery paths and crowded streets.

Cultural Sensitivity: This is a time for family. Be respectful of traditions. It is customary to offer well-wishes; learning a simple "Xinnian Kuaile" (Happy New Year) or "Gongxi Facai" (Congratulations and Prosperity) will be met with delighted smiles. It is also traditional to give and receive red envelopes (Hongbao) containing money, but as a tourist, you are not expected to participate in this. Always ask permission before taking photos of people, especially during their private celebrations.

Visiting Guilin during the Chinese New Year is a bold travel choice, but for those seeking an authentic, profound cultural experience amidst some of the world's most stunning scenery, it is an unparalleled adventure. The challenges of the crowds are far outweighed by the privilege of partaking in the most important celebration in the Chinese calendar. It’s a journey that will leave you with memories not just of beautiful landscapes, but of the powerful, joyful spirit of its people.
