Must-See Attractions in Guilin: A Traveler’s Checklist

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

Guilin is one of those rare places on Earth that seems almost too beautiful to be real. Nestled in the Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region of southern China, this city has inspired poets and painters for centuries with its dramatic karst mountains, winding rivers, and lush countryside. If you’re planning a trip to this part of the world, there are certain sights you simply cannot miss. This traveler’s checklist covers the essential attractions, along with some local tips and cultural insights to help you make the most of your journey.

The Natural Wonders: Guilin’s Iconic Landscapes

Guilin’s landscape is the stuff of legends—literally. It’s said that the city was created when a celestial being trod upon the earth, leaving behind the stunning peaks and rivers we see today. Whether you believe the myths or not, there’s no denying the magic of this place.

Li River Cruise: The Heart of Guilin

No trip to Guilin is complete without a cruise down the Li River. This isn’t just a boat ride; it’s a journey through a living painting. The stretch from Guilin to Yangshuo is particularly famous, offering views of mist-shrouded karst peaks, water buffalo grazing along the banks, and bamboo rafts drifting by. The cruise typically takes four to five hours, depending on the water level and type of boat you choose. For the best experience, opt for a bamboo raft in the quieter sections near Yangshuo. You’ll get closer to the water and have more opportunities for photos. Keep an eye out for local fishermen using cormorants to catch fish—a traditional method that’s become a symbol of the region.

Yangshuo: Where Adventure Meets Serenity

Once you arrive in Yangshuo, you’ll understand why so many travelers extend their stay here. This small town is surrounded by breathtaking scenery and offers a mix of relaxation and adventure. Rent a bicycle and explore the countryside, passing through rice paddies and tiny villages. For a more structured experience, join a guided tour to the famous Moon Hill, a natural arch with a hole through its center. If you’re feeling active, try rock climbing on the karst formations—Yangshuo is known as one of the best climbing destinations in Asia. In the evening, don’t miss the Impression Liu Sanjie show, a spectacular open-air performance directed by Zhang Yimou that uses the Li River as its stage.

Reed Flute Cave: A Subterranean Fantasy

Back in Guilin city, the Reed Flute Cave is a must-visit. This limestone cave is like stepping into another world, with stalactites, stalagmites, and rock formations illuminated by colorful lights. The name comes from the reeds growing outside, which locals once used to make flutes. Inside, you’ll walk through chambers with names like Crystal Palace and Dragon Pagoda, each more fantastical than the last. The cave is especially magical for children and those who love photography. Be sure to wear comfortable shoes, as the path can be slippery, and allow at least an hour to explore.

Cultural Gems: Temples, Towns, and Traditions

Beyond the natural beauty, Guilin is rich in history and culture. From ancient towns to spiritual sites, there’s plenty to discover for those interested in the human stories behind the scenery.

Longji Rice Terraces: A Masterpiece of Agriculture

About two hours from Guilin, the Longji Rice Terraces (also known as the Dragon’s Backbone) are a testament to human ingenuity. Built over 500 years ago by the Zhuang and Yao people, these terraces cascade down the mountainsides like steps for giants. The best times to visit are during the planting season in spring, when the fields are filled with water, and in autumn, when the rice turns golden. Hiking through the terraces is a rewarding experience, with trails ranging from easy walks to more challenging climbs. Stay overnight in a local guesthouse to fully appreciate the tranquility and enjoy homemade dishes like bamboo rice and stir-fried wild vegetables.

Sun and Moon Pagodas: Symbols of Harmony

In the heart of Guilin, the Sun and Moon Pagodas stand as iconic symbols of the city. Connected by an underwater tunnel, these pagodas represent the harmony between yin and yang. The Sun Pagoda is the world’s tallest bronze pagoda, while the Moon Pagoda is made of glazed glass. Visit at night when they are beautifully lit up, reflecting on the lake. You can climb to the top for panoramic views of the city, and along the way, learn about Buddhist culture and local history.

Fubo Hill: History and Panoramic Views

For a dose of history paired with stunning vistas, head to Fubo Hill. This hill is named after a general who supposedly cut off his own head to prove a point (don’t worry, it’s just a legend!). Today, it’s a popular spot for hiking and sightseeing. The path to the top is steep but manageable, and along the way, you’ll find ancient inscriptions and small temples. At the summit, you’re rewarded with a 360-degree view of Guilin and the Li River. It’s especially beautiful at sunrise or sunset.

Local Experiences: Food, Markets, and Crafts

Travel isn’t just about seeing sights; it’s about immersing yourself in the local culture. Guilin offers plenty of opportunities to taste, shop, and connect with the community.

Guilin Cuisine: A Feast for the Senses

Guilin’s food is as memorable as its landscapes. Start with Guilin米粉 (Guilin mǐfěn), the city’s famous rice noodles served in a savory broth with peanuts, pickled vegetables, and chili. For something unique, try啤酒鱼 (píjiǔ yú), a Yangshuo specialty of fish cooked in local beer. Street food is everywhere—don’t miss the stinky tofu or grilled skewers at night markets. If you’re feeling adventurous, sample some snake or frog dishes, which are local delicacies. Wash it all down with a glass of Osmanthus wine, made from the fragrant flowers that bloom throughout the city.

West Street: The Hub of Activity

In Yangshuo, West Street (Xi Jie) is the place to be. This pedestrian street is packed with cafes, bars, souvenir shops, and art galleries. It’s a great spot to people-watch, practice your bargaining skills, or simply enjoy a coffee while taking in the lively atmosphere. At night, the street transforms into a bustling night market where you can buy everything from handmade jewelry to traditional paintings. For a cultural twist, visit the calligraphy shops where artists will write your name in Chinese characters.

Traditional Crafts: Cormorant Fishing and Fan Making

To dive deeper into local traditions, consider joining a cormorant fishing demonstration. Though now mostly performed for tourists, this ancient technique involves fishermen using trained birds to catch fish. It’s a fascinating glimpse into the past. Another hands-on experience is learning to make Chinese fans. In workshops around Guilin, you can decorate your own paper fan with paintings or calligraphy—a perfect souvenir to take home.

Practical Tips for Your Guilin Adventure

To make your trip smooth and enjoyable, here are some practical suggestions based on current travel trends.

Best Time to Visit

Guilin is a year-round destination, but the best times are spring (April to June) and autumn (September to November). During these months, the weather is mild, and the landscapes are at their most vibrant. Summer can be hot and humid, while winter is cooler but less crowded. If you’re planning to see the Longji Terraces, avoid the rainy season in July and August when paths may be muddy.

Getting Around

Guilin is well-connected by air, train, and bus. Within the city, taxis and ride-sharing apps like Didi are convenient. For longer trips, such as to Yangshuo or the rice terraces, consider hiring a private driver or joining a tour group. Biking is popular in Yangshuo, and electric scooters are available for rent. Always negotiate prices for services like boat rides or guides to avoid overpaying.

Sustainable Travel Practices

As tourism grows, it’s important to travel responsibly. Support local businesses by eating at family-run restaurants and buying handicrafts directly from artisans. Avoid single-use plastics—bring a reusable water bottle, as tap water is not safe to drink. When hiking, stick to marked trails to protect the fragile ecosystem. By being mindful, you can help preserve Guilin’s beauty for future generations.

Beyond the Checklist: Hidden Gems

While the main attractions are unforgettable, don’t overlook Guilin’s lesser-known spots. For instance, the ancient town of Daxu offers a glimpse into rural life without the crowds. Or, take a day trip to the Lingqu Canal, an engineering marvel older than the Great Wall. These off-the-beaten-path experiences can make your trip even more special.
