Guilin Hiking: Trails with Ancient Trees

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

Guilin, a jewel in China’s Guangxi region, is renowned for its surreal karst landscapes, winding rivers, and lush greenery. But beyond the postcard-perfect Li River and bustling Yangshuo, there’s a quieter, equally mesmerizing side to Guilin: its hiking trails dotted with ancient trees. These trails offer a unique blend of natural beauty, cultural heritage, and tranquility, making them a must-explore for nature lovers and adventure seekers.

The Allure of Ancient Trees in Guilin

Ancient trees are more than just towering giants; they’re living witnesses to centuries of history. In Guilin, many of these trees are revered as sacred, often found near temples, villages, or along forgotten paths. Their gnarled roots and sprawling canopies create an otherworldly atmosphere, perfect for immersive hikes.

Why Hike Among Ancient Trees?

  • Historical Significance: Some trees are over 1,000 years old, with legends tied to local folklore.
  • Ecological Diversity: These trees support unique ecosystems, home to rare birds, insects, and flora.
  • Spiritual Connection: Many trails pass by shrines or temples where locals still pay respects to these natural monuments.

Top Guilin Hiking Trails with Ancient Trees

1. Yangshuo’s Moon Hill to Dragon Bridge Trail

This moderate 8-kilometer hike combines stunning karst views with pockets of ancient banyan trees. The trail starts at Moon Hill, a iconic limestone arch, and winds through rice paddies and small villages. Near Dragon Bridge, a 500-year-old banyan tree stands as a silent guardian, its roots intertwined with local legends.

Highlights:
- Panoramic views from Moon Hill’s summit.
- A hidden 200-year-old camphor tree near a tucked-away tea house.

2. Xingping’s Laozhai Mountain Trail

For those seeking solitude, the Laozhai Mountain trail near Xingping is a hidden gem. The path is lined with ancient sweet osmanthus trees, their fragrant blossoms perfuming the air in autumn. The hike culminates at a viewpoint overlooking the Li River’s most photographed bend.

Pro Tip: Visit in October to catch the osmanthus bloom—locals harvest the flowers for tea and traditional desserts.

3. Longji Rice Terraces’ Ancient Forest Walk

While the Longji Terraces are famous for their agricultural marvels, few explore the ancient fir and pine forests bordering the fields. A guided hike through these woods reveals trees planted by the Zhuang and Yao ethnic groups centuries ago, some marked with red ribbons for good fortune.

Don’t Miss: The "Dragon’s Backbone" viewpoint, where terraces and ancient trees merge into a breathtaking tapestry.

Lesser-Known Trails for the Adventurous

The Hidden Valley of Daxu Ancient Town

Daxu, a riverside town frozen in time, is the gateway to a secluded valley filled with towering gingko trees. A 5-kilometer loop trail takes you past Ming-era stone bridges and a 1,200-year-old gingko, its golden leaves creating a carpet every November.

Local Secret: Stop by a village home for a taste of guilin mifen (rice noodles) prepared by elderly residents—their recipes are as old as the trees.

Cat Mountain’s Mystical Banyan Grove

Near Guilin’s outskirts, Cat Mountain (Mao’er Shan) shelters a grove of banyans so dense it feels like stepping into a fantasy novel. The trees’ aerial roots form natural arches, and local guides share tales of spirits dwelling within.

Caution: The trail is slippery after rain, but the eerie beauty is worth the effort.

Cultural Encounters Along the Trails

Hiking in Guilin isn’t just about nature; it’s a cultural journey. Many ancient trees are tied to local traditions:
- Wish Trees: Near Yangshuo, travelers tie red ribbons to a 700-year-old tree, believing it grants wishes.
- Tea Under the Canopy: In Xing’an County, families serve tea beneath ancient tea-oil camellias, a practice dating to the Tang Dynasty.

Practical Tips for Hiking in Guilin

  1. Best Time to Visit: Spring (March-May) and autumn (September-November) offer mild weather and vibrant foliage.
  2. Guides vs. Solo: Remote trails like Laozhai Mountain benefit from local guides who know hidden tree groves.
  3. Respect the Trees: Avoid carving or touching bark—many are protected cultural relics.
  4. Pack Light but Smart: Carry water, bug spray, and a rain jacket—Guilin’s weather shifts quickly.

Preserving Guilin’s Natural Heritage

Deforestation and tourism pressure threaten some ancient trees. Organizations like Guilin Green Initiative work with villages to promote eco-tourism. Travelers can help by sticking to marked trails and supporting homestays that fund conservation.

For those yearning to walk among living history, Guilin’s trails with ancient trees offer a rare blend of adventure and serenity. Whether you’re photographing a millennia-old gingko or sipping tea in its shade, these hikes promise memories as enduring as the trees themselves.
