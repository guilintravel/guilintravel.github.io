Guilin’s Traditional New Year Celebrations and Customs

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

Nestled among the breathtaking karst mountains and winding rivers of southern China, Guilin is not only a paradise for nature lovers but also a treasure trove of rich cultural traditions. Among its most vibrant celebrations is the Lunar New Year, a time when the city comes alive with age-old customs, festive foods, and lively performances. For travelers seeking an authentic cultural experience, Guilin’s New Year festivities offer a unique blend of tradition and local charm.

The Heart of Guilin’s New Year Festivities

Family Reunions and Feasting

Like much of China, Guilin places immense importance on family during the Lunar New Year. Locals return to their hometowns, filling the streets with a sense of warmth and excitement. The reunion dinner, or tuánnián fàn, is the centerpiece of the celebrations. In Guilin, this feast often features regional specialties such as:

  • Guilin Rice Noodles (Guilin mǐfěn): A must-try dish, often served with pickled vegetables, peanuts, and chili.
  • Steamed Glutinous Rice Cake (niángāo): Symbolizing prosperity and growth in the new year.
  • Taro Duck (yùtóu yā): A savory dish combining tender duck with taro root, a local favorite.

Red Decorations and Lucky Symbols

Walking through Guilin during the New Year, you’ll see streets adorned with red lanterns, couplets (chūnlián), and paper cuttings. These decorations are believed to ward off evil spirits and attract good fortune. Markets buzz with vendors selling:

  • Fu Characters (福字): Often hung upside down to symbolize "fortune arriving."
  • Kumquat Trees: A symbol of wealth, commonly displayed in homes.
  • Firecrackers: Though restricted in urban areas, the sound of firecrackers still echoes in rural parts of Guilin to scare away bad luck.

Unique Guilin Customs You Won’t Find Elsewhere

Dragon and Lion Dances with a Local Twist

While dragon and lion dances are common across China, Guilin’s performances incorporate elements of local Zhuang and Yao minority cultures. Troupes dressed in vibrant costumes parade through the streets, accompanied by the rhythmic beats of drums and gongs. The lions often "pluck the greens" (cǎiqīng), snatching lettuce hung above doorways—a gesture symbolizing prosperity.

The "First Light" Ceremony on Elephant Trunk Hill

One of Guilin’s most iconic landmarks, Elephant Trunk Hill, becomes a focal point during New Year’s Eve. Locals gather by the Li River to witness the "first light" ceremony, where lanterns are released into the sky at midnight. The reflection of the lights on the water creates a magical scene, making it a photographer’s dream.

New Year Temple Fairs at Seven-Star Park

Seven-Star Park, Guilin’s largest scenic area, hosts lively temple fairs during the holiday. Visitors can enjoy:

  • Traditional Folk Performances: Including opera, acrobatics, and minority dances.
  • Handicraft Stalls: Selling everything from embroidered textiles to wooden carvings.
  • Fortune-Telling Booths: Where locals seek predictions for the year ahead.

How Travelers Can Experience Guilin’s New Year Magic

Stay in a Local Homestay

For an immersive experience, consider booking a homestay with a Guilin family. Many hosts welcome guests to join in making dumplings, writing spring couplets, or even learning traditional New Year songs.

Join a Food Tour

Guilin’s night markets, like Zhengyang Pedestrian Street, overflow with seasonal treats during the New Year. A guided food tour can introduce you to lesser-known delicacies, such as zòngzi (sticky rice dumplings wrapped in bamboo leaves) or sweet tangyuan (glutinous rice balls).

Take a River Cruise at Night

A Li River cruise during the New Year offers stunning views of illuminated karst peaks and floating lanterns. Some boats even host special dinners with live folk music.

Visit Longsheng’s Terraced Fields

Just a short trip from Guilin, the Longsheng Rice Terraces are especially picturesque in winter. The local Yao and Zhuang villages celebrate the New Year with bonfires, singing, and traditional costumes—a perfect side trip for culture enthusiasts.

Final Tips for Travelers

  • Book Early: New Year is peak travel season, so accommodations fill up quickly.
  • Dress Warmly: While Guilin’s winters are mild, evenings by the river can be chilly.
  • Respect Local Customs: Avoid wearing white (associated with mourning) and always accept red envelopes (hóngbāo) with both hands.

Whether you’re savoring a bowl of steaming Guilin mǐfěn, watching lanterns float down the Li River, or joining a lively dragon dance, celebrating the Lunar New Year in Guilin is an unforgettable way to experience China’s cultural heritage.
