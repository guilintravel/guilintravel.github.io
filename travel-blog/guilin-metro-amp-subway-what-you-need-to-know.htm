Guilin Metro & Subway: What You Need to Know

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

Guilin, a jewel in China's Guangxi region, has long captivated travelers with its ethereal karst mountains, serene Li River, and poetic landscapes that seem lifted from a traditional ink painting. For decades, the primary modes of transportation for tourists have been boats, buses, and bicycles. However, a new chapter is unfolding. The rumble of construction and the promise of a modern metro system are set to transform how visitors and locals alike navigate this ancient city. While not fully operational as a comprehensive subway network yet, the ongoing development of the Guilin Metro is a major point of interest and a glimpse into the city's future. This guide will navigate you through everything you need to know about this exciting transit evolution.

The Current State: More Than Just a Plan

Unlike the sprawling, decades-old metro systems in Beijing or Shanghai, Guilin's foray into rail transit is in its vibrant youth. It's crucial for travelers to understand that this is a system being built in real-time. The initial phases focus on a modern light rail transit (LRT) system, which will effectively function as an above-ground metro for most of its route.

Line 1: The Tourist Artery (Under Construction)

The first and most significant line for visitors is Line 1. This north-south corridor is designed to connect major transportation hubs with key urban and scenic areas.

  • Route: It is planned to run from the Guilin Liangjiang International Airport (KWL) in the south, through the city center, and north to the Guilin Railway Station (the main conventional train station). Crucially, a branch line is planned to extend to the Guilin North Railway Station (GBZ), the city's high-speed rail (HSR) hub.
  • Key Stops for Tourists: While the final station names may be adjusted, the route is designed to serve:
    • The city center, putting hotels, Zhengyang Pedestrian Street, and the Two Rivers and Four Lakes scenic area within easy reach.
    • Proximity to major bus stations for transfers to Yangshuo.
    • Direct access to both the airport and train stations, eliminating the need for taxis or buses for these transfers.

Line 2: The Future East-West Connector

Line 2 is in the planning stages and is envisioned as an east-west line that will intersect with Line 1, creating a crucial transfer point. This line will further open up the city, making it even easier to reach cultural sites and newer urban districts.

Why the Guilin Metro is a Game-Changer for Tourism

The introduction of a rail-based transit system is more than just a convenience; it's a fundamental shift that enhances the entire travel experience.

Seamless Airport and Train Transfers

This is the single biggest advantage. Upon arrival at Guilin Liangjiang International Airport, currently a 45-minute to 1-hour drive from the city center (longer in traffic), you will eventually be able to hop on a clean, efficient train. This will whisk you directly downtown or to your train connection at Guilin North Station without haggling with taxi drivers or navigating bus schedules. It brings a level of predictability and ease that modern travelers highly value.

Decongesting the Scenic City

Guilin's beauty is often masked by traffic congestion, especially during peak tourist seasons. The metro system offers a sustainable solution. By providing a reliable alternative to cars and tour buses, it will help reduce traffic and pollution, preserving the clean air and tranquil atmosphere that visitors come to enjoy. A less congested city means a more pleasant experience for everyone.

Unlocking New Neighborhoods

Fixed rail lines act as arteries for development. The metro will make it practical for tourists to stay in a wider range of neighborhoods beyond the immediate city center. This could lead to the growth of new hospitality hubs near metro stations, offering more accommodation options and potentially better value. It encourages exploration beyond the well-trodden tourist path.

What Travelers Need to Know for Their Trip (2024-2025 and Beyond)

Since the system is still under construction, managing expectations is key.

Timeline and Realistic Expectations

As of 2024, the system is not yet open to the public. Construction on major infrastructure projects like this is complex and can face delays. While there is optimism for initial phases to begin operation in the coming years, travelers planning trips in the immediate future should not base their itineraries on the metro being available. Always have a backup plan using traditional transport methods.

Navigating the Current Transport Ecosystem

Until the metro arrives, here’s how to get around Guilin effectively:

  • Taxis and Ride-Hailing: Apps like Didi (China's Uber) are widely used and very reliable in Guilin. They are affordable for getting across the city. Always ensure your driver uses the meter or that the price is fixed via the app.
  • Public Buses: Guilin has an extensive and incredibly cheap bus network. For the adventurous traveler, buses can get you almost anywhere. Key routes connect the city center with the train stations and major tourist sites. Have your hotel write down your destination in Chinese to show the bus driver.
  • Bicycles and E-bikes: For shorter distances within the city center, rental bicycles and shared e-bikes (accessed via apps like Meituan or Hello) are a fantastic and fun way to get around, especially for exploring the lakeside paths.
  • Tourist Boats: For a specific purpose—the Li River cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo—the boat is the primary and essential mode of transport. This is a scenic journey, not just a commute.

Beyond the Tracks: Tapping into Guilin's Enduring Magic

The metro will change how you arrive, but it won't change why you come. Guilin’s magic lies in its timeless landscapes and cultural experiences.

The Can't-Miss Experiences Accessible by Future Metro

Imagine a future trip where you exit a metro station and are a short walk or bus ride from:

  • Elephant Trunk Hill (Xiangbishan): The iconic symbol of Guilin, a hill that looks like an elephant drinking from the Li River.
  • Reed Flute Cave (Ludi Yan): A stunning natural limestone cave illuminated with multi-colored lights, creating an otherworldly atmosphere.
  • Seven Star Park (Qixing Gongyuan): The city's largest park, featuring gardens, caves, a zoo, and the famous Seven Star Peak.

The Food Scene: A Metro Food Crawl

The metro will revolutionize culinary tourism. You could design a food crawl along Line 1, hopping off at different stations to sample local specialties. Must-tries include:

  • Guilin Rice Noodles (Guilin Mifen): The city's most famous dish. Grab a bowl at a local hole-in-the-wall joint. The process involves first eating the noodles with toppings like peanuts, pickled vegetables, and chili, then drinking the broth afterwards.
  • Beer Fish (Pijiu Yu): A signature dish from the Yangshuo area, where fish is fried and then braised in local beer and spices.
  • Snail: A popular local snack, often stir-fried with chili and sour bamboo shoot.

The development of the Guilin Metro is a symbol of the city's dynamic spirit—honoring its ancient past while boldly stepping into the future. It promises to make one of China's most beautiful destinations more accessible, sustainable, and enjoyable for the global traveler. While you may need to rely on buses and taxis for your next visit, keep an eye on the progress. The day you can glide from the airport to the foot of a karst peak by train is coming, and it will make the magic of Guilin easier to discover than ever before.
