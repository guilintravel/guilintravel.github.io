Guilin’s Most Famous Statues and Monuments

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

The narrative of Guilin is often written in water and limestone. Travelers flock to the Li River, marvel at the Reed Flute Cave, and cycle through the dreamlike landscapes of Yangshuo. Yet, to understand the soul of this city, one must also learn to read the stone and bronze that punctuate its parks, hills, and riverbanks. These statues and monuments are not mere decorations; they are the silent narrators of Guilin’s history, the embodiments of its myths, and the focal points of its modern cultural identity. This journey goes beyond the natural scenery to explore the iconic figures and structures that have witnessed, and shaped, the story of Guilin.

The Sentinel of the City: Jingjiang Prince City & The Stone Carvings of Fubo Hill

Before the selfie sticks and tour groups, Guilin was a place of immense strategic importance. The remnants of this powerful past are most palpably felt in the northeastern part of the city.

Jingjiang Prince's Palace and its Guardian Kings

While the restored Jingjiang Prince's Palace (Jingjiang Wangcheng) itself is a sprawling complex, its most significant monumental features are the grand, weathered stone lions and mythical beasts that guard its gates and pathways. These are not the delicate statues of a poet’s garden; they are symbols of imperial authority and protection. Carved during the Ming Dynasty, they represent the era when Guilin served as the seat of a princely fiefdom. Walking through the Chengnan Gate (Solitary Beauty Peak entrance), you are traversing the same path princes and officials once did, flanked by silent stone sentinels that have witnessed centuries of ceremony, intrigue, and the rise and fall of dynasties. They are a monumental reminder that Guilin’s beauty was once fortified.

Fubo Hill: An Open-Air Museum of Stone

Just a short walk away, Fubo Hill (Fúbō Shān) rises dramatically from the ground. It is a natural monument in itself, but its true treasure lies in the hundreds of stone carvings and Buddhist statues etched into its caves and cliffs. This is where Guilin’s spiritual and literary history converges. As you explore the Returned Pearl Cave and the Thousand Buddha Cave, you'll encounter exquisite carvings of Buddha and Bodhisattvas dating back to the Tang Dynasty. Their serene faces, illuminated by the occasional shaft of sunlight, have offered spiritual solace for over a thousand years.

Interspersed among these religious icons are countless stone tablets bearing poems and inscriptions from famous travelers, officials, and poets across different dynasties. These are not just signatures; they are profound reflections on the landscape, personal musings, and historical records. In a very real sense, Fubo Hill is one of Guilin's oldest and most continuous blogs, with entries carved in stone instead of typed on a screen. The most famous statue here is the large, iron Buddha, but the collective impact of these countless smaller carvings is what makes Fubo Hill an unparalleled monumental site.

Poets in Bronze: The Cultural Heart of Guilin

If the stone carvings of Fubo are the historical manuscripts, then the bronze statues scattered throughout Guilin’s parks are the curated library of its cultural soul. These figures pay homage to the individuals whose words immortalized Guilin’s landscape long before any travel brochure existed.

The Sage of the Banyan Lake: Tang Dynasty Poets

Strolling around the tranquil Banyan Lake (Róng Hú) and杉 Lake (Shān Hú) area, you will inevitably come across life-sized bronze statues depicting celebrated poets. The most famous among them is likely the statue of Han Yu, the influential Tang Dynasty writer and poet who penned eloquent verses praising Guilin's unique scenery. These statues are often depicted in moments of contemplation or inspiration, quill in hand or gazing across the water towards the iconic Sun and Moon Pagodas. They have become more than monuments; they are interactive parts of the cityscape. It is a common and charming sight to see locals practicing Tai Chi nearby or tourists sitting beside a bronze poet for a photograph, creating a living connection between the city’s glorious literary past and its vibrant present.

The Legend of the Song Dynasty Official

Another significant figure immortalized in the city is Liu Zongyuan, a renowned poet and statesman of the Song Dynasty who was exiled to the region. While his time here was born of political misfortune, he contributed greatly to the local culture and infrastructure. Statues and references to him serve as a monument to resilience and the idea that one can find beauty and purpose even in adversity. His presence in Guilin’s monumental landscape underscores a deep respect for intellectual and moral integrity.

Modern Icons and the Spirit of the Li River

Guilin’s monumental narrative is not stuck in the past. The 20th and 21st centuries have introduced new icons that reflect evolving cultural values and the city’s global fame as a tourist destination.

The Seven-Star Park Celestial Steed

Within the vast expanse of Seven-Star Park (Qīxīng Gōngyuán), one modern monument captures the imagination: the statue of the Celestial Steed (Tuó Jiāng). This powerful, dynamic sculpture depicts a legendary horse emerging from the water, based on a local myth. It represents strength, vitality, and the magical spirit of the Li River. Its modern design provides a striking contrast to the ancient stone carvings found elsewhere in the same park, showing that Guilin’s story is still being written and that new legends can be forged in metal and placed alongside the old.

The Embroidered Ball and the Zhuang Culture

As you travel through the outskirts of Guilin and into the surrounding countryside, the monuments begin to reflect the rich culture of the Zhuang people, the largest ethnic minority in China. While not always singular, towering statues, you will often see monumental representations of the Embroidered Ball, a symbol of love and goodwill in Zhuang tradition. Large, colorful sculptures of these balls adorn cultural villages and public spaces. They are a vibrant, photogenic monument to the area's diverse ethnic tapestry and are a huge tourism hotspot, especially for visitors interested in cultural performances and marriage customs. They remind us that the monuments of Guilin are not solely about history and poetry, but also about living traditions, love, and celebration.

Monumental Experiences: Weaving Statues into Your Itinerary

Visiting these sites is more than just a checklist activity. To truly appreciate them, consider how they fit into the broader Guilin experience.

A perfect evening might start with a visit to the statues of the poets by Banyan Lake. As dusk settles, watch the city lights begin to glitter on the water, mirroring the thoughts these bronze figures once had. Then, take the short walk to the Sun and Moon Pagodas—monumental structures in their own right—and see them illuminated against the night sky. This creates a thematic journey through art, literature, and modern urban beauty.

For the history enthusiast, a dedicated day to the Jingjiang Prince City and Fubo Hill is essential. Climb to the top of Fubo Hill for a panoramic view of the city and imagine the generations of travelers who carved their names below. Touch the same stone that Ming Dynasty princes touched and feel the physical connection to the past.

No tour of Guilin's monuments is complete without acknowledging the greatest, most dynamic monument of all: the Li River itself. While not a statue, the river is the central, flowing monument around which all other history and culture revolves. The karst peaks that line its banks are natural monuments that have inspired every poet, artist, and emperor celebrated in the city's man-made creations. A Li River cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo is, therefore, a pilgrimage to the primary source of Guilin's legendary status.
