Weekend in Guilin: Best Places to Eat, Stay & Explore

  • Home|
  • Travel Blog|
  • Blog Details

The name Guilin has long been synonymous with China’s most breathtaking natural scenery. “East or west, Guilin landscape is best!” goes the old saying, and for good reason. Those iconic karst peaks, rising like jade hairpins from the misty Li River, have inspired poets and painters for over a thousand years. But Guilin is more than a postcard; it’s a living, breathing city where dramatic geology meets vibrant local culture, incredible food, and modern comforts. A weekend here is a whirlwind of sensory delights—a chance to hike through fairy-tale mountains, cruise past water buffalo and bamboo groves, and savor flavors unique to Guangxi. This guide is your blueprint for an unforgettable 48 to 72 hours, packing in the essential eats, the most rewarding stays, and the must-explore spots that go beyond the obvious.

Where to Stay: From Riverside Luxury to Charming Alleys

Choosing your base in Guilin is crucial. The city is relatively compact, but each area offers a distinct vibe.

Luxury on the Li River

For those seeking unparalleled views and top-tier service, the hotels lining the banks of the Li River in the city center are unmatched. Waking up to your private balcony overlooking the serene water with Elephant Trunk Hill in the distance is an experience in itself. These establishments often blend modern luxury with subtle Chinese architectural elements, featuring infinity pools that seem to merge with the river, world-class spas, and multiple dining options. They are perfectly positioned for evening strolls along the Binjiang Road promenade, where the city lights dance on the water. This is the choice for a romantic getaway or a treat-yourself weekend where convenience and scenery are paramount.

Boutique Charms in Zhengyang Pedestrian Street

If you crave immersion in local life and quirky charm, look for a boutique hotel or a well-reviewed guesthouse in or around the Zhengyang Pedestrian Street area. This vibrant, car-free zone is the heart of old Guilin, buzzing with activity day and night. Staying here means you’re steps away from snack stalls, souvenir shops, traditional tea houses, and lively bars. The accommodations in these converted old buildings often feature courtyards, wooden beam ceilings, and personalized service. The noise can be a factor, but for the energy and immediate access to street food and culture, it’s unbeatable. It’s also a short walk to the Sun and Moon Twin Pagodas and Ronghu Lake.

The Artist’s Retreat: Stay in Yangshuo

Here’s a hot take for your weekend: consider basing yourself in Yangshuo. While technically a separate town about 90 minutes from Guilin city by car, Yangshuo places you right in the epicenter of the most stunning karst landscape. If your primary goal is biking, hiking, climbing, and being surrounded by peaks, this is the move. The town itself is touristy but fun, with its famous West Street offering a global mix of food and nightlife. The surrounding countryside, however, is pure magic. Charming boutique hotels and rustic-chic resorts nestled among rice paddies and along the Yulong River provide a tranquil and profoundly beautiful setting. You can do your Li River cruise from here (starting from Xingping) and explore without the need to commute from Guilin city each day.

What to Explore: Peaks, Rivers, and Ancient Streets

A Guilin weekend requires strategic planning to balance iconic sights with hidden gems.

The Unmissable Icons

No first visit is complete without seeing the landmarks that define Guilin.

  • Li River Cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo: This is the crown jewel. The 4-5 hour scenic cruise down the Li River is a journey through a living Chinese scroll painting. You’ll pass the Nine-Horse Fresco Hill, Yellow Cloth Shoal, and countless unnamed peaks that stir the imagination. Book in advance and opt for the mid-morning departure to enjoy the light. The journey ends in Yangshuo, where you can spend the afternoon exploring before returning to Guilin by bus or car.
  • Elephant Trunk Hill (Xiangbishan): Guilin’s official city symbol. This hill naturally resembles an elephant drinking from the river. While you can pay to enter the park, one of the best views is actually from the free-to-walk Xiangshan Scenic Area on the opposite bank, especially at night when it’s lit up.
  • Reed Flute Cave (Ludi Yan): A spectacular natural limestone cave illuminated by multicolored lights. The 240-meter-long walk through otherworldly stalactite and stalagmite formations named “Crystal Palace” or “Dragon Pagoda” is a cool (literally) and fascinating activity, perfect for a hot afternoon.

Active Adventures & Local Life

To truly connect with the landscape, you need to get into it.

  • Biking the Yulong River in Yangshuo: Rent a bicycle and spend a half-day pedaling along the flat paths beside the serene Yulong River. You’ll cross ancient stone bridges, see farmers working fields, and have the karst peaks all to yourself. You can even combine it with a bamboo raft ride on a quieter, more intimate stretch of river than the bustling Li.
  • Sunrise at Laozhai Mountain: For the dedicated photographer and hiker, a pre-dawn trip to Laozhai Mountain (near Yangshuo) rewards you with what is arguably the most famous view in the region: a sea of mist swirling around countless karst peaks as the sun rises. It requires a short but steep hike, but the vista is unforgettable.
  • Explore Xingping Ancient Town: Older and less commercialized than Yangshuo, Xingping is postcard-perfect. Wander its ancient flagstone streets, see the old fishing village, and walk to the 20 RMB Note Viewpoint, where the landscape depicted on the back of China’s twenty-yuan bill comes to life.

Cultural & Evening Explorations

  • Sun and Moon Twin Pagodas: These beautiful pagodas, one in copper (Sun) and one in marble (Moon), connected by an underwater tunnel, are stunning when illuminated at night. A walk around Ronghu and Shanhu Lakes offers fantastic reflections and a peaceful urban escape.
  • Impression Sanjie Liu: Directed by famed filmmaker Zhang Yimou, this outdoor night show on the Li River in Yangshuo is a spectacle. Using the natural river and karst peaks as its backdrop, hundreds of local performers present a breathtaking display of light, song, and ethnic culture from the Zhuang, Miao, and Yao peoples.

Where to Eat: A Culinary Journey Through Guilin

Guilin’s food is a highlight, characterized by bold flavors, river fish, and local specialties.

Must-Try Local Dishes

  • Guilin Rice Noodles (Guilin Mifen): This is the city’s breakfast soul food. Don’t be fooled by its simple appearance. The magic is in the broth, slowly simmered with pork bones and spices. The ritual is key: you get your bowl of noodles with slices of cured beef, peanuts, and pickled vegetables. Eat the noodles first, then add broth to drink the soup. Chongshan Mifen and Shiwei Mifen are legendary local spots.
  • Beer Fish (Pijiu Yu): A Yangshuo classic. Fresh Li River fish is deep-fried until crispy, then braised in a savory, slightly sweet sauce made with local beer, tomatoes, chili, and garlic. It’s aromatic, flavorful, and best enjoyed with a group.
  • Snail-Stuffed Tofu (Luo Si Niang): A unique delicacy. Fresh river snails are minced and mixed with pork and herbs, then stuffed into fried tofu puffs or vegetables and stewed. The flavor is deep, earthy, and incredibly savory.
  • Oil Tea (Youcha): A savory, slightly bitter tea soup made by frying tea leaves with ginger, garlic, and oil, then boiling it. It’s served with puffed rice, peanuts, and scallions. It’s an acquired taste but a cherished tradition of the Dong and Yao minorities.

Best Areas & Restaurants to Savor the Flavors

  • Zhengyang Pedestrian Street & Shangshui Food Street: This is your one-stop-shop for street food adventure. Graze on stinky tofu, barbequed skewers, zongzi (glutinous rice dumplings), and sweet treats. The energy is infectious.
  • Chuanwei Gang (Flavor Lane): A local favorite for a more sit-down, authentic experience. This small alley is packed with tiny, family-run restaurants serving up incredible Guilin rice noodles, stir-fries, and hot pot. It’s bustling, noisy, and real.
  • Riverside Dining: For a memorable meal with a view, several restaurants along the Li River near Elephant Trunk Hill offer terraces. While the food can be more tourist-oriented, the ambiance at sunset is magical. For a higher-end local dining experience, look for restaurants like Xiao Nan Guo which serve excellent interpretations of regional classics in a comfortable setting.
  • Craft Beer & Coffee in Yangshuo: Reflecting its international traveler scene, Yangshuo has developed a surprising craft beer and café culture. After a day of biking, enjoy a locally brewed pale ale at one of the microbreweries on West Street, or find a cozy café for a flat white.

A weekend in Guilin is a journey that engages all your senses. It’s the feeling of mist on your face during a river cruise, the satisfying slurp of rice noodles at a bustling stall, the awe of watching the sun set behind a silhouette of peaks, and the peaceful rhythm of a bicycle ride through timeless countryside. By blending the iconic sights with active exploration and deep culinary dives, you’ll leave with more than just photos—you’ll carry the essence of this landscape poetry with you.
